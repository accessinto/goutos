The name of Greece differs in Greek compared with the names used for the country in other languages and cultures, just like the names of the Greeks.

The ancient and modern name of the country is Hellas or Hellada, and its official name is the Hellenic Republic "Heliniki Dimokratia".

In English, however, the country is usually called Greece, which comes from the Latin Graecia (as used by the Romans) and literally means 'the land of the Greeks'.

The Name of Greece
wikipedia mobile

A long long time ago, the Romans conquered Hellas. The Romans spoke Latin, and the Latin name for the Hellenic people (Graicias) comes from a common Hellenic name, "Graikos".

The Romans also conquered Gaul, and Latin became the language of the Gallic people. The Gallic people stopped calling themselves by that name and started using "Franks", and later, "French", speaking their own dialect of Latin called "French".

In 1066AD, a French conqueror named William the Conqueror conquered England, and it became cool among English speakers to use French words, like "Greece".

That is why we call the Greeks Greek, not Hellenic. It's because we use the Latin root, not the Greek root.

Why call Greece like that ?
